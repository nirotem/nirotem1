Research Projects

Here is a selection of projects I am currently working on. They all connect to the theme of human rights, global norms, and the principles that are integral to a free society—including their contestations, inner tensions, and institutional measurement. Feel free to reach out if you have any questions or ideas for collaborations!

Ongoing Research

Illiberalism, Higher Education, and Academic Freedom

This project implements insights from sociological neo-institutionalism to understand the growing global trend of the illiberal backlash. I examine the connection between pressures on academic freedom and distrust in science globally. Using cross-national survey data, I analyze whether individuals' beliefs align with or decouple from the policies adopted by their countries, identifying science as a contested terrain in liberal societies and a locus of consensus in illiberal regimes.

Computational Culture in Science

In collaboration with Liron Shani, I analyze the cases of the life sciences and digital humanities to study how patterns of scientific knowledge are changed by technology and the new social interactions that emerge. We argue that computation is more than a technical toolkit; it entails a distinct mode of thinking that is fostering an emergent culture that cuts across the traditional science-humanities divide.

Scientific Productivity in US Universities

Working with Keith Goldstein and Gad Yair, we unpack the inner workings of American science using a unique dataset covering the publication output of faculty in hundreds of research universities. We evaluate the "three cultures hypothesis," showing that the natural sciences, social sciences, and humanities operate in distinct cultural worlds with highly different productivity norms.

Completed Research

The Dropout Study

For several years I served as a CO-PI (with Gad Yair) in a student attrition study at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem (funded by The Edmond de Rothschild Foundation, Israel). With data going back a decade, we utilized machine learning models to investigate the phenomenon of student dropout at both the undergraduate and master’s degree level. We encountered the riddle of existential dropout, namely, those students who leave despite an adequate academic record. Subsequently, we explored the Israeli program of affirmative action and found that students admitted through this route had a similar ratio of graduation as the general student population. Thus we called upon universities to open their gates wide. Lastly, we turned our attention to the objective and subjective reasons for M.A. students’ departure before completion of their degree.

Illiberalism and Judicial Independence

This study focuses on the global erosion of judicial independence (1960-2018). Using cross-national and longitudinal panel data, I track the global shift towards illiberalism and examine the effect of illiberal intergovernmental organizations on state activity. The findings reveal a growing divide and the formation of a complex mesh between liberal and illiberal norms in contemporary global society.

Humanitarianism & the UNHCR (Dissertation)

My doctoral research conducted a historical ethnography of the United Nations High Commissioner for Refugees (UNHCR) using extensive archival work in Geneva. I tracked flows of knowledge to pinpoint changes in the humanitarian field and recognized the infiltration of ideas from international development and human rights, arguing that contemporary world society is a "fusion society" characterized by a cultural multiplex.

The "Other" Other: Anti-Semitic and Anti-Muslim Views

In collaboration with Joseph Gerteis, I analyzed survey data to study the relationship between anti-Semitic and anti-Muslim views in America. Using nationally representative survey data (Boundaries in the American Mosaic survey), we explored these forms of ethno-religious exclusion and found strong correlations with political and religious affiliations.

Women's Empowerment in Sub-Saharan Africa

Together with Elizabeth Heger Boyle, I examined how women enact global scripts of women's rights in low- and middle-income countries. Using survey data from 25 countries, we focused on women's empowerment in household decision-making and attitudes toward partner violence, highlighting the interaction between world society norms and localized religious institutions.
